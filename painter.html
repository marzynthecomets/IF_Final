FRACTIONS:

Fractions discuss dividing up a whole into parts, like how Mondrian did his paintings. If you divide a whole number into parts and then combine them, you will likely need fractions. The numerator tells you how many of something you have and the denominator tells you how many pieces of that thing that you have. Ex: ⅞ = Out of 8 total pieces, we have 7 of them.

Limit Applications: An interesting application of a puzzle similar to mondrian’s is if you take a square and keep dividing it into ½ sized slices - so ½, ¼, ⅛…. Until you can’t anymore. This gives us what’s called a limit in calculus if you add up all the pieces - even if we keep slicing we won’t exceed the limit of 1 whole (which is your original square). A famous version of this puzzle is Archimedes and the Turtle.

An extra challenge: If you want a more challenging version of the Mondrian Puzzle above, check out this puzzle from mathcircles.com, “ You’re Mondrian’s mathematical boss. Instead of allowing Mondrian to randomly draw rectangles and colors -you lay out requirements: 1) Mondrian must cover an N by N canvas entirely with rectangles. 2) Every rectangle in the painting must have different dimensions. 3) Mondrian must use as few colors as possible, and rectangles with the same color cannot touch one another. Under these rules, Mondrian must try to minimize his score. A painting’s score is the area of its largest rectangle minus the area of its smallest rectangle. What is the highest score you can achieve?”
