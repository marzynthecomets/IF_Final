But First, Some Math By Haley Horton

The basic idea of percentages is that we have something “out of 100.”

Some Etymology: This comes from the Latin “per” meaning of or out of; and “cent” meaning 100. Think: Century, Dollars and Cents.

With that in mind, there is a group of percents that are special - we call them benchmark percents. They are: 1%, 10%, 20%, 25%, 50%, 75% and 100%. We are going to look at 10%, since it’s a very common building block to make other harder percents.

Example 1: If I have 20 apples and I want, that means that I want 10/100 or 1/10 or “one for every 10”. So I would want 2 apples. So, 10% = 2.

Example 2: If I have 40 apples and I want 10%, that means that I want 10/100 or 1/10 or “one for every 10”. So every 10 apples, I keep 1. I do this 4 times, leaving me with 4 apples.

Shortcut!

Conveniently, my answer can also be found by dividing my original number (ex: apples) by 10. So from my above examples: 20/10 = 2 40/10 = 4

Another way of looking at it is that I can move the decimal by one place value to the right, giving me 10% of 20.0 = 20/10 = 2.0 10% of 40.0 = 40/10 = 4.0. The best part about this pattern is that it works for any number.

So if I have $134.76, to find 10%, I can divide by 10 OR I can move my decimal place one to the left, which gives me $13.47.

Extension: 20% is 2 times 10%. Try and find 20% of 40. (hint: it’s 2 x 4).
